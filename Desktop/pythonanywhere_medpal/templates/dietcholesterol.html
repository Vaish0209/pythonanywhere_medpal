Diet for lowering cholesterol


1. Legumes
  • Legumes, also known as pulses, are a group of plant foods that includes beans, peas and lentils.
  • Legumes contain a lot of fiber, minerals and protein. Replacing some refined grains and processed meats in your diet with legumes can lower your risk of heart disease.
2. Apples
  • A small 2019 studyTrusted Source found that among 40 participants with mildly high cholesterol, eating two apples a day reduced both total and LDL cholesterol levels. It also lowered levels of triglycerides, a type of fat in the blood.
3. Nuts — Especially Almonds and Walnuts
  • Nuts are another exceptionally nutrient-dense food.
  • They’re very high in monounsaturated fats. Walnuts are also rich in the plant variety of omega-3 fatty acids, a type of polyunsaturated fat associated with heart health
  • Almonds and other nuts are particularly rich in L-arginine, an amino acid that helps your body make nitric oxide. This, in turn, helps regulate blood pressure
  • Calcium, magnesium and potassium, also found in nuts, may reduce blood pressure and lower your risk of heart disease.
4. Fatty Fish
  • Fatty fish, such as salmon and mackerel, are excellent sources of long-chain omega-3 fatty acids.
  • Omega-3s bolster heart health by increasing “good” HDL cholesterol and lowering inflammation and stroke risk.
    • 5. Whole Grains — Especially Oats and Barley
    • Oats: Contain beta-glucan, a type of soluble fiber that helps lower cholesterol. Eating oats may lower total cholesterol by 5% and “bad” LDL cholesterol by 7%
    • Barley: Also rich in beta-glucans and can help lower “bad” LDL cholesterol
    6. Fruits and Berries
    • Many types of fruit are rich in soluble fiber, which helps lower cholesterol levels
    • One kind of soluble fiber called pectin lowers cholesterol by up to 10%. It’s found in fruits including apples, grapes, citrus fruits and strawberries
    • It does this by encouraging your body to get rid of cholesterol and stopping your liver from producing this compound.
    • Eating berries and grapes, which are particularly rich sources of these plant compounds, can help increase “good” HDL and lower “bad” LDL cholesterol
    7. Dark Chocolate and Cocoa
    • Cocoa is the main ingredient in dark chocolate.
    • In one study, healthy adults drank a cocoa beverage twice a day for a month.They experienced a reduction in “bad” LDL cholesterol of 0.17 mmol/l (6.5 mg/dl). Their blood pressure also decreased and their “good” HDL cholesterol increased
    • Therefore, you should use cocoa alone or choose dark chocolate with a cocoa content of 75–85% or higher.




    8. Garlic
    • It contains various powerful plant compounds, including allicin, its main active compound
    • Studies suggest that garlic lowers blood pressure in people with elevated levels and may help lower total and “bad” LDL cholesterol — although the latter effect is less strong
    9. Avocados
    • Avocados are an exceptionally nutrient-dense fruit.
    • They’re a rich source of monounsaturated fats and fiber — two nutrients that help lower “bad” LDL and raise “good” HDL cholesterol
    • Clinical studies support the cholesterol-lowering effect of avocados.
    10.Tea
    • While green tea gets a lot of attention, black tea and white tea have similar properties and health effects.

      • Two of the primary beneficial compounds in tea are:

    • Catechins: Help activate nitric oxide, which is important for healthy blood pressure. They also inhibit cholesterol synthesis and absorption and help prevent blood clots
    • Quercetin: May improve blood vessel function and lower inflammation

      • Though most studies associate tea with lower total and “bad” LDL cholesterol, research is mixed on its effects on “good” HDL cholesterol and blood pressure
